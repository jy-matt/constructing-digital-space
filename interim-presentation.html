CONSTRUCTING DIGITAL SPACE

"It is likely that in the near future, a large number of people will be spending a considerable amount of time 'inside' such worlds. If we are to live - even partially - 'inside' these worlds, we must consider their architecture. The nature of space in VEs, however, is fundamentally different from the nature of real space and consequently the architecture of VEs will require a new theory and practice." - Bourdakis & Charitos, 1999

CONCEPT

Architecture and Virtual Environments

Despite the fact that virtual environments (VEs) have been around for more than 20 years and there has been continued research into their design and implementation, they have been regarded largely as entertainment and as accessories to physical interaction. In the midst of the post-digital revolution and the ongoing pandemic, we are increasingly looking at VEs as substitutes for real-life interaction, whether it be for social reasons, education, work, or leisure.

In that light, it is more important than ever to consider the design of virtual spaces in relation to the human experience. Within virtual environments, traditional architectural conceptions of scale, spatiality, movement, framing and interaction take on vastly different forms, yet designing virtual space is left largely to the purview of software developers, concept artists and game designers. Given the increasingly prevalant role of virtual environments in our daily lives, as well as the emergence of new technologies such as VR and AR, the spatial design of VEs deserves to be examined through an architectural lens.

"Architects need to start thinking about virtual reality as not just a tool, but as an end medium in itself." - Brett, 2015

Desire for Physicality

Virtual communication platforms can be assessed on a spectrum of spatiality and modes of interaction, from collaborative documents, text chat, virtual whiteboards, voice chat, or video chat, to more fully featured environments such as 2D, isometric, or 3D game-like simulations, or even fully VR platforms. Especially in most of the non-spatial platforms, most would agree that something is lost when transitioning from the physical to the virtual. Without people and items occupying a physical space, the perception of meetings and events is changed. In the digital realm, out of sight often means out of mind.

Within these virtual platforms there is an obvious desire for some form of tangibility and sense of space. How do we design virtual spaces conducive to interaction that aren't just carbon copies of physical space?

  1. What are the different rules that govern spatial interaction in virtual environments?
  2. In what ways can we take advantage of virtual platforms to go beyond replicating the physical?
  3. Can virtual environments take on a less detailed / more abstracted form, yet offer a satisfying level of spatial and physical immersion?

PLATFORM

There are two possible mediums for the exploration of virtual environments - a "web app" or game-environment operating in 2D screen space, or the use of VR technology which immerses the user entirely within the 3D space. After some background research and testing, I have decided to with VR despite the increased difficulty, due to the much greater potential that the platform offers.

Web App

  • Accessible
  • Potential for interesting conclusions
  • Well researched since late 90s
  • Confined to screen space, less spatial opportunity
  • Possibly becoming less relevant

Virtual Reality

  • Newer technology - less well explored
  • Spatial implications are much more relevant
  • Less accessible for presentation
  • Harder to test and build environment

PROGRAMME

At its core, creating a spatial experience in virtual environments is useful in several ways:

  1. Social Function - it is easier to meet and interact when you can understand relative positions in space
  2. 3D Content - interactions with content that are necessarily 3-dimensional, such as architectural models, large documents, physical items, museums, etc
  3. Art and Entertainment - spatial environments are of course important for 3D experiences such as games, entertainment, interactive media

Educational Focus

In order to limit the scope of the project and work towards a more defined goal, I have decided to focus on the design of learning environments. That is, any usage which involves multiple actors as well as the need to impart knowledge through the use of multimedia. This would target two of the three functions of virtual space as outlined above.

Such learning environments would include archetypes such as classrooms, lectures, conferences, groupwork, study spaces and so on.

PRECEDENT

The application of Architecture in virtual environments has been studied since the late 90s with the advent of rudimentary 3D graphics. However, the virtual world as we now understand it has a clear precursor - Cinema - which theorists and filmmakers have already studied in depth since the early 20th Century.

Architecture and Film

Cinema as a series of framed experiences - drawing from the heritage of the Greek picturesque in Architecture. Every experience within the virtual environment is necessarily framed by the boundaries of the screen space, making the curation of views and angles even more important.

Within film as well as virtual environments, spatial perception is bounded by the screen space . It is impossible to 'feel' the space around you without actively looking up or around. What are the implications of space-creation under these constraints?

Architecture as an inattentive form of viewing, whereas Cinema involves attentive viewing. In a way, VEs lie somewhere between the two - there is a conscious attention devoted to experiencing the virtual environment, yet with enough exposure the environment is likely to fade into the background as well.

Virtual Environment Design

Virtual environments have in fact been studied for over 20 years, with papers examining the various aspects of VE design in early 3D environments. I am currently in the process of conducting exhaustive research on the available literature, to see what can be applied to current issues, what frameworks can be adapted or referenced, as well as what issues might already have been investigated.

  • Design Precedents for Virtual Worlds (1996)
  • Virtual Environment Design - Defining a new direction for architectural education (1999)
  • Design in Virtual Environments Using Architectural Metaphor (2003)
  • A Methodology of Design for Virtual Spaces (2008)
  • Challenges in Virtual Environment Design: An Architectural Approach to Virtual Spaces (2008)
  • Architects in the Design of Virtual Reality Spaces (2016)

A virtual environment can through the visual dimension of perception, in combination with interactive feedback, be helpful in generating immersiveness, understood as the sensation of actually being present in an architectural space. A sensation of being present, even while one is maintaining a conscious knowledge that this feeling is only an illusion created by a digital model. - Hermund et al. 2018

PRELIMINARY INVESTIGATION

The smaller preliminary study aims to investigate the experience of spatiality in virtual reality environments. Building upon previous research that has investigated the perception of virtual space, this research project will focus on designing for human interaction and learning. Although there are many possible parameters to explore (as seen below), I will be focusing on extracting design principles that govern virtual user interaction. The results of this study will form a set of design guidelines for virtual learning spaces which will then be used for the final product.

Navigation

Testing movement from point A to B, or user orientation within complex spaces. How easily can users navigate different types of spaces within virtual space?

Extra-Diegetic Elements

In film and game design, diegesis refers to elements that belong to the narrative space such as characters, props, set, sound, etc, while extra- or non-diegetic refers to elements which are outside of the scene, such as soundtracks, overlays, or interface elements. How useful are extra-diegetic cues in the understanding of virtual space? Is it possible to do without them?

Modes of Movement

Especially in full VR environments, teleportation is a common method of transportation due to the disorientation associated with walking in-game but standing still in real life. What implications does this have on the way that we perceive and understand the sequence of spaces?

Information

What aspects of spatial design affect transmission of information? 3D space mainly allows for the layout of information in a spatial manner, making it easier to understand and organise large sets of data.

Understanding the potential of spatial information presentation

  1. Viewing information in screen-space
  2. Physically-imitative information viewing
  3. Hybrid viewing - extra-diegetic

Designing spaces for information viewing

How do we design environments where large amounts of information or media can be easily viewed and understood?

Interaction

Another major consideration in the design of spaces would be the facilitation of interaction between participants. Again, the conception of what is 'human scale' and distance is uncertain in the virtual environment.

Number of users

Possibility of rooms and environments to adapt to the number of users in a space, creating different settings for different purposes.

Distance between users

How does the distance between users affect perception of space and interactions? Does it actually matter given that communication can transcend space? Usually, people prefer to be up close to see facial expressions and hear better, but without such concerns in virtual space, what kind of "personal space" do users require?

Medium of interaction

Variety of possible modes for interaction - text, voice, other visual cues. Possibility of using spatial features as cues for interaction? E.g. walls block sound, distance modulates sound, line of sight, etc.

Navigation between groups

Tying in to the above idea of navigation - interactions between various groups can be represented in physical space using overlays, perspectives, or switching between spaces.

THESIS OUTCOMES

The final thesis outcome will be an interactive platform for learning that allows users to construct their own virtual environments, conforming to the principles highlighted by the earlier investigations. The use of these constructed environments would range from the temporal (meeting rooms) to the semi-permanent (classrooms, museums, conferences).

App Flow

  1. Users choose from a set of pre-made environments or create their own within an intuitive, front-end editor interface.
  2. Users 'playtest' their environment to see if it suits their needs - aim for minimal playtesting due to intended ease of use and modularity.
  3. Resulting virtual environment can be used by multiple participants for learning and discussion.

REFERENTIAL SPATIALITY

Most VR apps appear to have a richly detailed physical space as an anchor for users, likely to orientate them to the unfamiliar environment in the same way that skeumorphic design serves to acclimatise new digital users. Possibility of using such spaces as 'hubs' or anchors, while specialised rooms might be more abstract.

REPRESENTATION

Representations of objects, data and links are entirely spatial, taking the form of floating windows or interactable 3D objects. The ability to manipulate objects also helps greatly with the sense of immersion in the virtual environment.

NAVIGATION

Another major consideration in the design of spaces would be the facilitation of interaction between participants. Again, the conception of what is 'human scale' and distance is uncertain in the virtual environment.

Room-Scale

Walking or room-scale navigation provides the greatest immersive capacity, however it can also be more tiring and stressful for participants. The space requirement for room-scale navigation is also not necessarily accessible.

Controller

Controller movement scores fairly high on immersion and is easy to use, however it has a propensity to induce motion-sickness especially if you are not used to it.

Teleportation

Teleportation is the easiest and most convenient, however it scores lowest for immersiveness.
